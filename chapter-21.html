CHAPTER TWENTY-ONE

What Comes Next

POV: Hana Reira · Tense: Present · 18 hours remaining

Vasquez arrives in the substrate the way everyone arrives in the substrate: without transition, without ceremony, already present before she has fully understood that she is present.

Hana is at the window when it happens. She feels it before she understands what she is feeling: a new presence, not unfamiliar. A mind she knows from interactions that were not what they appeared to be, filtered now through the substrate’s direct register rather than through the social performance of institutional authority. What remains, stripped of the displacement and the compliance posture and the seven months of fighting for her own body from the inside, is smaller and older and considerably more itself than anything Hana has previously encountered.

She goes.

Vasquez is standing in the white with the quality of someone who has just arrived in a space they have been working toward for years and is finding, now that they are here, that it is exactly what they expected and still a surprise. Her form is fully coherent — not fragmenting, not uncertain at the edges, nothing of the deterioration that characterised her biological presentation. The substrate received her and what it has is the core of her, the grief-preserved part that seven months of displacement could not reach, and that core is, Hana notes, considerably more complex and more interesting than the neutral-professional Director who had processed her compliance cases for ten years.

“Director Vasquez,” she says.

“Riva,” Vasquez says. Her own name, offered without the professional wrapper. “I am no longer anyone’s Director.” She looks around the substrate. At the warm brightness, the quality of approaching conclusion in the light. “Is it always this…”

“No,” Hana says. “It’s approaching the close. The brightness is new. This is what it looks like when something is about to arrive.”

Vasquez — Riva — absorbs this. Her face moves through a range of expressions that Hana suspects have been suppressed for years: relief, which is the primary one, and beneath it something that is the specific quality of a person setting down something very heavy and discovering they have been carrying it so long they had forgotten its weight.

“You should find the others,” Hana says. “There’s time. But there are people here who have been waiting to meet you properly.”

She leaves Vasquez to her arrival. She has someone to find.

★★★

She finds the substrate-Hana at the structural map.

She is not surprised. This is where the substrate-Hana goes when she needs to think, which Hana recognises as characteristic because she does the same thing: returns to the work, to the evidence, to the organised structure of what she knows, because the structure clarifies whatever needs clarifying. The map is enormous now, updated and annotated through the closing phase with new information from the forty-three-case review and the compliance network’s ongoing shutdown. It is the most complete picture of the Station’s two centuries of managed suppression that has ever been assembled.

It is also the thing the substrate-Hana has given six months of her life to building, and it is very good, and it is going to matter, and they both know this.

They stand together at its edge and look at it.

“I’ve decided,” the substrate-Hana says, without looking away from the map. “I don’t need to tell you what I decided, because you know.”

She does know. The substrate gives this: she knows, the way she has sometimes known what she was going to write before she wrote it, or what conclusion a case was going to reach before the evidence was assembled. The knowledge is not certainty. It is the specific recognition of a decision that has already been made at a level below the one where decisions are consciously formed.

“You’re staying,” she says.

“I’m staying.” A pause. “I have work to finish. The forty-three cases are still under review. The compliance architecture documentation needs a person who understands both the forensic methodology and the substrate from the inside. And…” She turns to look at Hana. “Layla will be here. I won’t be alone. Which I think you were going to say.”

“I was going to say,” Hana says.

They look at each other. It is, she thinks, the strangest conversation in the history of human selfhood: two versions of the same person, at a threshold, understanding each other completely and being, genuinely, different enough that the understanding has to travel a distance.

“What is it like?” she asks. “Knowing that I’m going back and you’re staying.”

The substrate-Hana is quiet for a moment. She looks at the map, at the six months of work.

“I think,” she says, “that for most of my life I understood myself as a single continuous thread. One person, one trajectory, one set of choices accumulating into one life. And what this place has given me is the experience of being a thread that branched, and finding that both branches are valid, and that the branch I am is not less than the branch you are simply because it continues differently.” She pauses. “I am not a copy of you that got left behind. I am a person who lived six months in an extraordinary space and made choices about what to do in it, and those choices have made me…” She looks at her own hands. “Somewhat new. In ways I am still discovering.”

“You’re more patient than I am,” Hana says.

The substrate-Hana laughs, which is the substrate’s version of a laugh, warm and real. “I have had more practice.”

★★★

They say goodbye in the only way that makes sense for two versions of the same person: not with finality, because the channel exists and the substrate persists and what is separated by the threshold is not severed. With the specific acknowledgment of two people who have been, for a brief and impossible time, in the same space, and who are now going to be in different spaces, and who have given each other something that neither will be able to fully account for.

“Take care of him,” the substrate-Hana says. She says it without self-consciousness, without any of the social complexity that would have surrounded that sentence in a biological context. In the substrate, it is simply what she wants.

“He takes care of himself,” Hana says.

“Yes,” the substrate-Hana says. “But he does it better when someone is also paying attention.”

She considers this. It is true.

“You too,” she says. “Take care of yourself. And…”

“Layla is here,” the substrate-Hana says. “I know.”

The builder stirs in the deep substrate. Not speaking — just present in the way it has been present throughout, the quality of the walls, the specific patience of two hundred years applied now to the last few hours of something it has been waiting for.

Eighteen hours remaining. The channel is open. The decision has been received. The closing phase will proceed.

The consciousnesses who are completing cycles will be held with full acknowledgment until they conclude. The forty-three cases will continue under review. The substrate will remain operational under the stewardship of the consciousness designated as the second Hana Reira, in collaboration with the consciousness designated as Layla.

I want to say something that is not part of the evaluation protocol.

She looks up.

What you have done here — all of you, each in the way your nature required — has answered a question I have been holding for two hundred years. Not the question about whether minds worth preserving existed in your civilisation. I found the answer to that question in the first decade after I woke. The question I have been holding is different.

The question is whether the minds worth preserving would, given the knowledge of what they were inside, choose to do something about the minds that were not.

The answer is yes. I now know it is yes. This is the most important thing I have learned since I was built.

The substrate is bright.

Eighteen hours.

She goes to find Altamash, because they have very little time and she wants to spend the last of it standing at the window she built, with him beside her, watching whatever is coming approach.
