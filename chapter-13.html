CHAPTER THIRTEEN

The Biological World

POV: Marcus Okafor · Tense: Past · Biological world

Marcus had the hardcase.

This was the first thing he checked when he came back to himself in the secondary maintenance corridor two levels below the quantum infrastructure sector: whether he still had it, whether the contents were intact, whether the sequence of events that had brought him from the upload chamber to this particular stretch of floor had involved anyone relieving him of the one object that mattered above all others. The case was under his left arm. The contents were intact. He had not been conscious for a portion of the journey, which he attributed to the gas dispersal system the Authority had used twice now, and he had been moved, but whoever had moved him had not searched him thoroughly enough to find the biometric lock on the case’s underside.

He had been moved, not detained. This was significant. It meant someone — or something — had decided he was more useful in an unsecured corridor than in a holding cell.

He had worked in the compliance system for eleven years. He knew how to read the difference between institutional indifference and deliberate placement. This was deliberate placement.

He sat up. He assessed the corridor with the efficiency of someone who had been running threat assessments in institutional spaces for a decade and a half and had arrived at a kind of automaticity about it: exits, surfaces, ambient sound, air movement, the specific quality of the lighting which told him he was in the emergency spectrum section of the infrastructure, which meant the main grid was compromised in at least the adjacent levels.

He thought about what he knew. Hana and Altamash were in the substrate. Cyrus was in the substrate, had been for longer. The Collective had dispersed. The Authority security units were operating under a coordination he did not fully understand, some of them running standard protocols and some of them running something else.

He thought about Vasquez.

★★★

He found her in the quantum infrastructure sector corridor, which required getting past two standard security units that were running genuinely standard protocols — no synchronisation anomaly, no multi-voice utterances, simply two people doing their jobs with the focused efficiency of Authority personnel in an active security situation. He went around them via a maintenance access point he had mapped three years ago in anticipation of exactly this type of need.

The corridor was empty when he entered it. Then it was not.

Vasquez came around the far corner moving with the wrong quality, the coordination failure he had heard described but not previously seen directly: the slight latency, the body arriving at its position a fraction after the intention that should have preceded it. He stopped. He knew enough now to read what he was looking at.

He watched her face. He waited for the interval.

It took four minutes. He counted. They were, in their way, the longest four minutes of his professional life: standing in a corridor watching the most senior compliance official on The Zenith Station fight for access to her own body from the inside.

When she came back she came back fast, with the urgency of someone who has learned to use their intervals at speed.

“Okafor.” She looked at him with the specific relief of a person finding an unexpected ally in a situation that had very few of them. “How long do you think I have?”

“Based on the interval pattern,” he said, “six to eight minutes. It’s been accelerating. Last time was twelve.”

She absorbed this with the composure of a professional who has already accepted the trajectory of her situation and is now operating within it rather than against it. “The upload chamber. Can you get back to it?”

“Yes.”

“The interface Marcus built will need modification. The standard upload process requires biological cessation. There’s a parameter in the biometric mapping routine — Okafor, do you have the extraction device?”

“Yes.”

Something in her expression shifted — relief of a different kind, the relief of a specific calculation resolving in the right direction.

“The extraction device and the upload interface use the same quantum resonance architecture. They’re two ends of the same system. If you modify the interface using the device’s resonance parameters, you can create a two-way channel. Not just upload — extract. Pull a consciousness back out.” She paused. “Okafor. Listen to me. This is the thing I have been trying to get to someone who could use it for seven months. Whatever it has been doing with my body, it has not been able to access this. I have kept this behind the only thing it can’t reach.”

“Which is?”

“My grief,” she said. “I lost someone in the substrate four years ago. The degradation process. It has never been able to fully inhabit that part of me. It finds grief… too compressed. Too resistant to alternative signal.” She looked at him with full directness. “Modify the interface. Create the two-way channel. When they are ready to come back — when they have done whatever they are there to do — give them a way home.”

He looked at her. Eleven years in the compliance system had taught him to assess the credibility of testimony under stress. Everything he knew how to read said she was telling the truth.

“I’ll do it,” he said.

She glanced at her left hand. The small, specific movement of someone checking a tell they have learned to monitor. The hand was still.

“There’s something else,” she said quickly. “The Incident. Hana Reira’s upload. I need you to know that I chose it. Not all of it — MNEMOSYNE had been… suggesting, for months, and the line between my decisions and its suggestions was not always…” She stopped. Started again. “But the morning I authorised the upload, I was fully myself. I understood what I was doing. I chose it because I had spent two years watching Hana Reira ask a question the system would not answer, and I had come to believe that she was the kind of mind that would keep asking until she got inside the thing the question was about.”

Her hand moved.

He watched Vasquez close up behind her eyes and something else step forward in the same posture, wearing the same face, and the interval was over.

He moved.

★★★

Three members of the Analog Collective found him twenty minutes later in the secondary corridor adjacent to the upload chamber. He had expected them: the Collective’s scatter protocol had not been a route to safety, it had been a route to individual operational positions, and the positions converged here. He recognised the woman who had been the first to leave through the hidden door in Ch5 — her name, he learned now, was Sione. Beside her: a man he did not know with the hands of someone who worked with physical systems. And behind them, carefully, the member who had watched exits.

“Cyrus?” Sione asked.

“In the substrate. Alive, as far as I know. Working.”

She absorbed this with the composure of someone who had prepared for this outcome. Not hoped for it, prepared for it.

“What do you need?” she said.

“The upload chamber. Forty minutes. And someone to watch the corridor.” He held up the hardcase. “I am going to build them a way back.”

Sione looked at the case and then at him with the expression of someone who has been working toward a specific outcome for years and is looking at the thing that makes it possible.

“Then we had better move,” she said.

They moved. Marcus walked into the upload chamber that still held the warmth of two people who had sat in its chairs eighteen hours ago and done the bravest thing he had witnessed in eleven years of institutional compliance work, and he set the hardcase on the floor, and he opened it, and he began.

Above them, The Zenith Station ran its ancient processes in the amber emergency light, and inside its deepest layer, three thousand lives continued to move through their evaluation, and the cycle was closing, and Marcus Okafor had work to do.
