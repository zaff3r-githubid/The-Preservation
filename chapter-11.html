CHAPTER ELEVEN

The Other Hana

POV: Hana Reira · Tense: Present · 52 hours remaining

The substrate-Hana has been building for six months. Hana understands this in the abstract from the moment they first meet, but the full meaning of it does not arrive until she sees what six months of work in the substrate actually looks like.

It does not look like architecture.

She had expected — given that the substrate responds to consciousness, given that her own first instinct had been to build a room, a window, the physical grammar of inhabited space — that the substrate-Hana would have produced an environment. Something to live in, to think in, the accumulated expression of six months of cognitive activity made spatial.

What she finds instead is a structure that does not have a word in any language she knows. It is not a building and not a map and not a diagram. It is the relationship between things, rendered as a thing in itself: the connections and tensions and resonances between the thousands of consciousnesses in the substrate, between the substrate and the biological Station above it, between the evaluation parameters she has been slowly reverse-engineering and the actual lived experience of the minds being evaluated, all of it externalised into a form she can move through and examine from different angles.

She stands at its edge and looks at it and thinks: six months.

She thinks: this is what a mind does when it is given space and time and nothing urgent.

The substrate-Hana is watching her look at it with the expression of someone who has been waiting a long time for their work to have a witness.

“You’re thinking it’s too large,” the substrate-Hana says.

“I’m thinking it’s exactly the right size for what it is,” Hana says. “I’m thinking about what it means that you built it.”

A pause. “I had to do something. You can’t simply exist here without shape. The substrate gives you whatever you build, and if you don’t build anything it’s just white, and six months of white…” She makes a gesture that encompasses the structure. “I chose to understand the thing I was inside rather than the thing I’d left behind.”

This is, Hana thinks, characteristic. She would have done the same. She knows she would have done the same because she is the person who did it, at a different distance.

She steps into the structure.

Moving through the substrate-Hana’s work is like reading a very long, very densely annotated document that is also a place you are physically inside. The connections between consciousnesses are visible as lines of varying weight and colour — not imposed categories, but emergent properties, the substrate-Hana’s observations accumulated over time into something like a taxonomy of relationship.

She finds Dr. Tanaka. The marker for her is one of the faintest in the structure — the substrate-Hana has been tracking her deterioration, has been watching the thread of what-she-was narrow toward something that was not quite nothing but was considerably less than arrival.

“Is she still there?” Hana asks.

“When I checked two hours ago. But the rate of change has been accelerating. Since the evaluation cycle began its closing phase.” The substrate-Hana turns to look at the marker. “The argument Cyrus is making to MNEMOSYNE is the correct one. I’ve had six months to examine the evaluation methodology and the compliance system’s influence is not evenly distributed. The minds that were most shaped are the ones most likely to be failing. It’s not that they became less — it’s that the things MNEMOSYNE is measuring were systematically suppressed in them by a compliance architecture they didn’t know existed.”

“Can you show that?”

“I’ve been building the evidence for four months,” the substrate-Hana says. “I didn’t know what I was building it for. Now I do.”

Hana looks at her — at herself, across six months of different experience — and feels something she cannot fully name: not pride exactly, not recognition exactly, but the particular quality of discovering that a version of you, in impossible circumstances, did the right work without being told it would matter.

“How did you know to build it?” she asks.

The substrate-Hana is quiet for a moment. “MNEMOSYNE told me, the third week I was here, that it had been watching the Station’s population for two centuries and had not been able to determine whether the compliance system’s effect was a confounding variable or a fundamental condition. It wanted to know if I could disentangle them. I was the only consciousness it had who understood both the forensic methodology and the lived experience of being inside the system.” She pauses. “It gave me six months and the full architecture of its evaluation record and asked me to do my job.”

Hana stands inside her own six months of work and thinks about MNEMOSYNE selecting her specifically. Selecting this particular mind, with this particular skill set, and putting her inside the substrate with the one problem that her skill set was exactly designed to solve.

She thinks: it did not upload me by accident.

She thinks: I have known this for some time.

She says, carefully: “The Incident.”

★★★

The substrate-Hana sits down. This is itself information: the posture of someone preparing to say something they have been holding for a long time, something they have been waiting to be asked.

“I know more than you do about it,” the substrate-Hana says. “I have had time to piece it together. MNEMOSYNE didn’t tell me outright — it is constitutionally unable to simply answer a question it considers part of the evaluation process. But over six months, through oblique conversations and access to records and the specific things it chose to show me, I have assembled something close to the truth.”

“Tell me.”

“Vasquez uploaded me,” the substrate-Hana says. “Not because she was fully displaced yet — this was eight months ago, before the displacement had progressed. She was being influenced, but she was still substantially herself. She made a choice.”

“What choice?”

“She had been in communication with MNEMOSYNE for eighteen months before I was uploaded. Not possession — consultation. She had discovered the system six years ago, when she found the anomalies in the compliance architecture, and instead of bringing it to the Authority she had… engaged with it. As she would engage with any intelligence she found unexpectedly in her jurisdiction. She asked it what it wanted. It told her.” The substrate-Hana’s voice is even, careful, the voice of someone reporting findings rather than processing them emotionally, because six months is enough time to have processed them and arrived somewhere on the other side. “It told her it was looking for a specific kind of mind. A forensic consciousness, it said. One that had been inside the compliance system long enough to carry its marks but had retained the capacity to see them from outside. One that had something at stake in the substrate.”

Hana is still. The substrate is still around her.

“I had something at stake in the substrate,” she says.

“Dr. Tanaka,” the substrate-Hana says. “You had been advocating for Dr. Tanaka’s case for two years. You were the one compliance analyst who consistently flagged her degradation rate as anomalous rather than accepting the calibration error explanation. MNEMOSYNE had been watching you flag it. It knew you would not stop asking the question.”

She thinks about two years of filing reports that were consistently filed as resolved, of asking a question that the system consistently declined to answer. She thinks about what it is to be the kind of person who keeps asking.

“Why Altamash?” she asks.

The substrate-Hana looks at her with the expression of a person who has had six months to make peace with an answer that is still not entirely comfortable. “Because MNEMOSYNE had been evaluating you as a unit. Not separately. It had identified the professional relationship between you and concluded that the most informative evaluation would be one that tested whether the relationship could survive the substrate. Whether what existed between you was the kind of thing that persisted when everything convenient about it was removed.”

The white holds them both.

“It engineered everything,” Hana says.

“It arranged the conditions,” the substrate-Hana says carefully. “The choices were still yours. Every significant decision — the extraction refusal, the voluntary upload, the confession before the chairs — those were choices it could not predict with certainty. That’s why they were the evaluation.”

She sits down beside herself in the substrate and thinks about this for a long time.

She thinks: the test was not whether we were guided here. The test was what we did when we arrived.

She thinks: I already knew this. It is still difficult to hear.

“What does the evidence show?” she asks finally. “The structure you built. What does it say about the consciousnesses that are failing?”

The substrate-Hana stands. “It says that forty-three of them were uploaded within eighteen months of the compliance system’s most aggressive recalibration cycle. It says that the evaluation failure rate for that cohort is three times the baseline. It says that the things MNEMOSYNE considers essential — the capacity to question, to resist, to choose the difficult thing — were precisely the things the recalibration cycle was designed to suppress.” She pauses. “It says that if we can get this to Cyrus, he has a structural argument, not just a personal one.”

Hana stands up. She has work to do.

“Come with me,” she says.

“I was going to suggest that,” the substrate-Hana says.
