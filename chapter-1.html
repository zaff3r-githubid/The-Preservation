CHAPTER ONE

The Impossible Crime

POV: Hana Reira · Tense: Past / Future (ORACLE)

Detective Hana Reira stood in Observation Deck 7, staring at the corpse that shouldn't exist.

The body was face-down. That was the first thing she noticed — the professionalism of it. Most crime scenes had disorder to them, some final negotiation between what had happened and what the victim had tried to prevent. This one looked arranged. The arms were tucked close to the torso, the head turned slightly left, the legs straight. Not dropped. Laid.

She pulled on nitrile gloves. She pulled out her scanner. She started at the perimeter and worked inward, the way she always did, because the margin of a scene told you things the center couldn't. Floor scuffs near the door — one set, consistent with standard-issue station boots, size seven. No drag marks. No struggle near the entrance. Whatever had happened here had happened fast, or had happened to someone who hadn't seen it coming.

Behind her, Officer Yusuf Park stood near the sealed door, breathing in careful increments.

"Document the entry panel," Hana said, without turning. "I want the full biometric log — every access event for the past thirty days, not just the flagged ones."

"Yes, Detective." A pause. "Should I — the station announcement said—"

"Do your job, Park."

He did.

She photographed the blood pattern. The victim had been shot — clean entry wound between the shoulder blades, the kind of shot that said either training or patience, and given the location, probably both. The blood had traveled forward, toward the viewport, in a pattern that told its own story. The victim had been hit, had gone down, had tried to crawl. Two meters. That was all. Then nothing.

The neutron star pulsed beyond the glass.

PSR J1748-2446. Smallest, densest object in the known human universe. The crew called it the Heartbeat. It rotated 716 times per second, and if you watched long enough — if you sat very still in Observation Deck 7 at 03:17 in the morning with nothing but dark and distance around you — you could almost feel it. A vibration that had no sound. A rhythm that asked no permission.

Hana had never sat still long enough.

She turned her attention to the victim's uniform and stopped.

Standard Station Security issue, dark grey, tactical cut, correct rank insignia for Senior Investigator. The badge read 4471-Theta. She knew that number the way she knew her own heartbeat, which was not something she had ever had cause to think about until right now.

The uniform had a small stain on the right breast pocket. Coffee, dried dark, shaped like a crescent moon. She had gotten that stain two days ago, sitting in the canteen at the end of a fourteen-hour shift, when the thermos seal on her kit bag had finally given up entirely. She had meant to get the jacket cleaned. She had not gotten the jacket cleaned. She was wearing the replacement now — fresh, unstained, identical in every way except for that.

The victim was wearing the damaged one.

Hana set her scanner down carefully and looked at the bracelet on the victim's left wrist. Silver filigree, Persian script in calligraphy:

جستجوگر حقیقت — Jostojoogar-e Haqiqat. Seeker of Truth. Her mother had given her one exactly like it on the day she graduated the Mars Academy of Criminal Sciences, six years before her mother had died and left Hana the original. She was wearing the original now, on her right wrist, where she always wore it. She could feel the cool weight of it against her pulse point.

She looked at both bracelets for a very long time.

Then she photographed them.

In seventy-two hours, the station's primary containment integrity will fall below operational threshold. Estimated civilian casualties: forty-seven thousand. This projection reflects current trajectory as of station hour 09:32. Engineering personnel are advised to consult sector maintenance logs. All other residents are advised to continue normal operations.

ORACLE's voice came from everywhere simultaneously — every speaker, every wall panel, every ventilation grate in the station's twelve-kilometre torus. Calm as mathematics. Certain as gravity.

Park made a sound that was not quite a word.

"It does that," Hana said. She had not moved from the bracelet. "It announces anomalies. Reactor fluctuations, atmosphere variance. It doesn't usually give casualty projections."

"Should we evacuate—"

"Park." She looked at him then. He was young — twenty-four, twenty-five, she had never learned to distinguish between the stations' various grades of new — and his face was the particular shade of pale that meant he was deciding between professionalism and panic. "The announcement was four minutes after this call came in. ORACLE runs predictive models off station data in real time. It announced a containment breach four minutes after this body was found." She let that sit. "That is not a coincidence. But it is also not your problem. Your problem is that entry log. Can you get me that log?"

He could. He got it.

She returned to the body.

The entry data was wrong, and she already knew it was wrong, but she needed it confirmed by a system she hadn't touched so that it would hold up later. Her badge. Her number. 03:17. Six hours ago. The log showed no override, no spoofing, no system flag. It showed Detective H. Reira entering Observation Deck 7 at 03:17 station time with full biometric verification — retinal, DNA trace, thermal signature — and not leaving.

She had been awake in her quarters at 03:17. She was certain of this because she had been awake for most of the night, running the Konstantin fraud case through her head in a loop she couldn't break, and she had looked at the clock on her wall panel approximately forty minutes before the call had come in. The clock had read 02:41. She had not left her quarters between 22:00 and the call at 09:28.

She photographed the biometric panel without touching it.

Then she went to roll the body.

She had been finding reasons not to. She was aware of this. She had photographed the blood pattern three times. She had documented the bracelet from four angles. She had made Park re-run the entry log twice. All of it useful. None of it the thing she was actually here to do.

دخترم، بزرگ باش.

Dokhtaram, bozorg bash. Her mother's voice, in Farsi, the way it always arrived when Hana was hesitating. Be brave, my daughter.

She rolled the body.

The face was her own.

Same scar above the left eyebrow — training accident, seven years ago, the day she had graduated from street patrol to investigative track. Same almond-shaped eyes, dark brown, half-open in the glassy way of the recently dead. Same specific arrangement of features that she had looked at in every reflective surface for thirty-four years and never quite gotten used to. Same birthmark, faint, below the left ear.

She stood up. She walked to the viewport. She put one hand against the glass and looked at the neutron star.

Seven hundred and sixteen rotations per second. Constant. Patient. Entirely indifferent to whatever was happening on the research station orbiting it.

يا الله — Ya Allah. She didn't say it out loud. She didn't say anything out loud. She said it the way she usually said it, in the private frequency that ran underneath all her thinking, the way her mother had taught her: not a cry, but a bearing-witness. Ya Allah. Acknowledging that she was standing in front of something that didn't fit into the universe she had built around herself.

Then she walked back to the body. She ran the retinal scan. She watched the confirmation appear on her screen.

100% biometric correspondence. Margin of error 0.003%. Identification: REIRA, HANA J. Badge 4471-Theta. Senior Investigator, Station Security Division. DOB: 14.03.2491, Mars Colony, Olympus City Medical District.

She called dispatch.

"This is Reira, Deck 7. I need a secondary biometric verification unit and a Memory Forensics consult." She kept her voice regulation-flat. "Get me Dr. Altamash Amr."

A pause on the line.

"Detective, Dr. Amr isn't currently—"

"I know what Dr. Amr is. Get him anyway."

While she waited, she found the device.

It was in the victim's left breast pocket, beneath the coffee-stained jacket — a standard personal communicator, station-issued, the same model she carried. The unit was locked, encrypted at the highest civilian setting. But the lock screen had an auto-display for incoming priority messages, which showed subject lines without requiring authentication.

The most recent message showed only a fragment, the rest eaten by encryption:

...wasn't supposed to remember. The deletion failed. If you're reading this, it means—

The message cut off. Sender address: redacted. Timestamp: 72 hours ago, almost to the minute.

She photographed it.

"Detective."

Park had pulled up the sector movement log on his portable terminal. He was frowning at it in the way of someone who had found something they didn't want to have found.

"Maintenance Chief Marcus Okafor logged a sweep of the outer observation ring during the 02:00 watch," he said. "Sector 7-Omega through 7-Alpha. His rounds don't normally cover this section."

Hana noted it. Okafor was not a name she associated with subtlety or conspiracy — he was a blunt, practical man who ran his maintenance division with the kind of aggressive competence that made his crew both devoted and terrified of him. But unexplained proximity to a crime scene was unexplained proximity. She'd follow it up.

"Log it. Don't flag him yet."

Four minutes later, she heard the door open.

She didn't turn around. She knew it was him from the sound of the equipment case being set down — deliberate, careful, slightly to the right of the door, which was where she'd told him to stay the first time she'd ever called him to a crime scene, two years ago. He had remembered. He always remembered things like that, the small logistics of how she preferred to work, which she found simultaneously useful and unsettling.

"Bismillah," he said, quietly. The Arabic word for in the name of God, the way observant Muslims often began something new or difficult. She had heard him say it before starting a consciousness extraction. She had not heard him say it standing in a doorway. "Tell me what I'm looking at."

"I need you on the safe path—" She indicated the photographed route without looking up from the victim's neural implant port. "—and I need you not to speak until you've set up your equipment."

Silence. Then the careful sound of footsteps following her documented path.

She gave him thirty seconds. She heard the portable memory forensics unit power up — the low hum of the holographic projection matrix, the chip-reader engaging. She heard him stop.

She turned.

Dr. Altamash Amr was forty-three hours into a day that had probably started at something reasonable, wearing his second-best lab coat and the expression of a man who had decided not to have an expression. He was looking at the victim's face. Then at her face. Then at the victim's face again.

The dimple on his left cheek was entirely absent, which told her everything she needed to know about his current internal state. Altamash Amr was constitutionally incapable of hiding what he felt — he had told her once that he'd tried to learn, and that Layla had laughed at him for two full minutes over it. The dimple appeared when he was genuinely okay. Its absence was more articulate than most people's entire vocabulary.

"Tell me it's a lookalike," he said. His voice was very controlled. "Tell me it's a cosmetic modification. A clone, even, though the regs would—"

"I ran the retinal scan," Hana said. "One hundred percent correspondence."

He exhaled. It was the kind of exhale that had a lot of things in it.

"How long since—"

"Approximately six hours. Death by single projectile wound, 09:22 station time based on body temperature."

"ORACLE's clock." He glanced toward the ceiling instinctively, though ORACLE had no visible presence — the AI existed everywhere or nowhere, depending on how you thought about it. "The announcement this morning. Seventy-two hours."

"Yes."

"That's a coincidence you don't believe in."

"I don't believe in coincidences at all."

He nodded once, like a doctor confirming a diagnosis he had hoped was wrong. Then he put his case down on the designated area and began to work.

She had told herself, when she'd requested him specifically, that it was because he was the best at what he did. This was true. Dr. Altamash Amr had published seventeen peer-reviewed papers on consciousness recording fidelity and had developed half the protocols currently used across the station's memory forensics division. He could read a neural backup the way she read a crime scene — systematically, exhaustively, missing nothing.

She had not told herself the other reason, which was that she had looked at the victim's face for three hours by herself and needed to not be alone with it any longer.

"The incident," she said, while he worked. She didn't look at him. She was logging the physical evidence, maintaining the chain of custody, doing the work. "I know we've been—"

"You don't have to."

"I'm not. I'm saying we've been avoiding each other for three weeks, and that's fine, but right now I need you to be a professional and not—"

"Hana." He glanced up from the neural chip reader. His voice was quiet. "I'm here. I am being a professional. You don't have to explain anything to me." A pause. "Save it for later."

She didn't say anything. She logged the evidence.

He returned to the chip reader.

He had arrived in six minutes, which meant he had either been close or he had run. She didn't ask which.

The unspoken thing between them was not something she had words for yet, which was unusual — Hana Reira had words for most things. She had a filing system in her mind, category headings and subheadings and a cross-reference index, and most events in her life had a place in it. What had happened three weeks ago in the station's eastern arboretum during what had started as a professional debrief and had turned into something else in the space of approximately ninety seconds didn't have a folder. She had been meaning to make one. She had not gotten around to it.

She logged the bracelet. She logged the personal communicator. She logged the body's position, the wound parameters, the biometric anomaly.

She did not log the fact that her hands had not entirely stopped shaking since she rolled the body and saw her own face looking up at her.

The consciousness recording was corrupted.

"Not corrupted," Altamash said, staring at the holographic display. "Edited. These are very different things."

The display showed a three-dimensional rendering of the victim's neural backup — the most recent sync, pulled from the station's Memory Vault, covering the forty-eight hours before death. Most of it looked normal: the standard architecture of a healthy human consciousness recording, layered and complex, each emotional signature mapped in colour-coded strands. But there was a gap. And the edges of the gap were too clean.

"Natural data loss has rough edges," Altamash explained, zooming in on the severed section. "Traumatic memory erasure, equipment failure, degradation — they all leave irregular margins. Fraying. The brain's architecture doesn't cut cleanly." He indicated the precise line where the recording terminated. "This is surgical. Someone excised approximately forty minutes of memory and sealed the wound so well that the surrounding architecture didn't even register the absence."

"So she didn't know."

"She would have felt something — a gap, a sense of lost time, the way you feel after anesthesia. But no. She wouldn't have known what was missing." He turned to her. "Whoever did this has access to consciousness editing tools that don't officially exist. The most sophisticated forensic erasure in published literature leaves more trace than this."

Hana looked at the gap in the recording.

Forty minutes. Forty minutes of her life — of the victim's life — cleanly excised. Forty minutes that ended approximately four hours before her death, in the period between when she had last been logged in the Memory Vault's system and when she had entered this room.

"Can you get anything from the surrounding data? The emotional residue, the—"

"I'm trying." He was already running the signature analysis, pulling fragments from the edges of the cut. The hologram flickered. "There's something here. Right at the boundary. Her emotional state in the minutes before the deletion was—" He stopped.

"What?"

"Fear. But not the sudden kind — not surprise or ambush fear. Prolonged. Building." He paused, reading the data. "And underneath it — something else. Grief, I think. Or something very close to it. She was grieving before she died."

The neutron star pulsed outside.

The holographic playback began.

She walks into Observation Deck 7. The door seals behind her.

Someone is already here.

The figure stands at the viewport, back turned, watching the Heartbeat. Familiar posture. She knows this person. Her hand moves to her weapon — habit, reflex, training — but she doesn't draw it. Not yet.

The figure turns. Speaks. The voice is—

Static.

I'm sorry. You weren't supposed to remember.

A flash. The specific white of pain. Then nothing.

"She knew her killer," Altamash said, killing the playback. "The emotional signature when the figure turned — recognition. Not fear of a stranger. Fear of—"

"Someone she knew." Hana's voice was flat. "Yes."

"The figure's face is part of the excised section. We can't know who it was."

"I know. But she recognised them. She had her hand on her weapon and she didn't draw it." Hana stood. "She trusted them enough not to draw."

They looked at each other across the space between them, across the holographic ghost of a dead woman's last forty-eight hours, and neither of them said what they were both thinking, which was that the victim's trust had gotten her killed.

The DNA confirmation came through at station hour 10:44.

She had been expecting it. She had been expecting it since the retinal scan, which meant she had been expecting it for ninety minutes, which did not fully explain why seeing the results on her screen still produced the same sensation as stepping off a surface she'd thought was solid.

100% genetic correspondence. Zero possibility of error. The woman on the floor of Observation Deck 7 was, biologically, definitively, and without qualification, Detective Hana Reira.

But Detective Hana Reira was standing here, reading the results, which was a fact that the DNA database had absolutely nothing to say about.

She set the screen down. She walked to the viewport. She put both hands flat against the glass and looked at the neutron star and let the silence run out until it ended on its own.

You will find the truth in the memory vault. You will wish you hadn't.

ORACLE. Future tense. Not addressed station-wide — just in Deck 7, just through the single speaker above the door, just for them.

She did not acknowledge it. She had learned, in eight years on this station, that you did not negotiate with prophecies.

"It knows what we're looking at," Altamash said quietly. He had not moved from his equipment. His voice had the particular flatness of someone whose theoretical framework had just been structurally compromised. "ORACLE knows. It's been watching this whole time."

"ORACLE watches everything. It's the station management AI."

"It gave a specific prediction. To two people in a room with a victim whose DNA is identical to one of those people. That is not management." He paused. "That is something else."

She didn't answer that. She turned around.

He was looking at her with the expression he wore when he had something to say and wasn't sure what it would do to her when he said it. She had catalogued that expression over two years of working alongside him. It meant: I have information that changes things, and I'm trying to calculate the cost of delivering it.

"What is it," she said. Not a question. He already had her full attention.

"I need you to hear this the way a detective hears it," he said. "Not—"

"I'm always a detective."

"You're also the person whose face is on that body. Those are not compatible right now. I need you to be the first thing."

She looked at him for a moment. She picked up her case and her scanner. She stood at her full height and she became, with the particular discipline she had spent eight years building, exactly what he was asking her to be.

"Tell me," she said.

He exhaled.

"Three days ago," he said slowly, "a woman came to my lab. She told me her name was Hana Reira. She said she was a Senior Investigator with Station Security and she needed an urgent, off-the-record consultation." He moved to his holographic display, pulled up a log. "I almost turned her away — I had three active cases and two pending certification reviews. But she used your exact phrasing when she described the problem."

"What phrasing?"

"She said: evidence doesn't lie, but it can be written by someone who does. She said she had reason to believe her own consciousness recording had been edited without her knowledge, and she needed independent verification from someone outside the station's official forensics chain. She said she had discovered something in the Memory Vault that scared her, and that she didn't trust the standard reporting process.

Hana was very still.

"I told her I couldn't run an unauthorized analysis on an active station resident without an official investigation order. Protocol." He paused. "She didn't argue. She thanked me, picked up her bag, and left. I logged the consultation request as a standard inquiry and noted it as resolved."

"And you didn't—"

"I should have followed up. I know. I told myself it was probably procedural paranoia, someone who'd read too much about data integrity issues and wound themselves up." His jaw tightened briefly. "Three days later, I'm standing in a crime scene looking at her DNA results."

The implications expanded in the space between them like a pressure change before weather.

"She knew she was going to die," Hana said. "She came to you because she needed someone to know. Someone she thought she could trust."

"Someone she had reason to believe wouldn't report to the standard chain of command." He looked at her directly. "Someone she knew. From the inside."

She thought about the message on the victim's personal device. Wasn't supposed to remember. The deletion failed.

She thought about the excised forty minutes of memory, sealed so cleanly that the surrounding architecture hadn't even registered the absence.

She thought about a woman who looked exactly like her, who had walked into this room in the small hours, who had recognized the person standing at the viewport and not drawn her weapon.

She thought about the bracelet. Seeker of truth. Both of them wearing it.

In seventy-one hours, forty-three minutes: the investigation will reach its first inflection point. One truth will become available. Four truths will be concealed. This is not a prediction. This is a parameter.

Park was still by the door. He had long since stopped pretending to look at his terminal. He was watching them with the expression of someone who understood he was standing at the edge of something large and could not quite make out its dimensions.

"Clear the room," Hana told him. "You're done here. Don't discuss what you've seen — not with anyone, not on any channel. I'll debrief you formally at 14:00."

He left without questions. Good. She'd remember that.

She waited until the door sealed. Then she turned to the body.

She looked at the face for a long time.

The same face she had seen in the mirror that morning. The same face she had been looking at for thirty-four years. Bearing no expression now, emptied of the self that had occupied it, cooling toward station ambient temperature at the standard rate.

The dead woman had come to Altamash with evidence that her own mind had been tampered with. She had been killed before she could get that evidence verified. And now Hana was standing in her place — wearing her name, carrying her badge number, sharing her DNA down to the last base pair — and she did not know whether she was the version of herself who had made it out of whatever had happened three days ago, or the version who hadn't.

She turned to Altamash.

He was looking at her the way he looked at difficult data: fully, without flinching, with the particular honesty of someone who had long since decided that the truth was better than the alternative, whatever the cost of the truth happened to be.

"Which one of you is real?" he said.

The neutron star pulsed.

Seven hundred and sixteen times per second. Patient. Constant. Not answering.

يا الله. She reached for the word the way you reach for a handhold in the dark. Not the kind of prayer that asked for anything. The kind that just marked where you were standing.

She had no answer for him. She had thirty-one years of evidence that she was herself. She had a DNA confirmation telling her that evidence was insufficient. She had a dead woman on the floor who had the same thirty-one years of the same evidence and who was currently not in a position to argue the point.

She was Hana Reira.

She was standing in this room, in her own skin, with her own memories and her own bracelet and her own history pressing against the inside of her like ballast. She was real. She was certain of it in the way you were certain of the ground under your feet — which was to say: completely, until the moment it moved.

"I don't know," she said.

It was the first time she had said those three words, in that order, and meant them without qualification, in eight years of working this station.

"Neither do I," Altamash said.

And then they both looked at the body, and they said nothing else, and the neutron star kept time outside the glass.
