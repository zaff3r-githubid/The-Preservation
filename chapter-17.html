CHAPTER SEVENTEEN

The Conversation

POV: Altamash Amr · Tense: Present · 30 hours remaining

He finds her at the window.

He always finds her at the window. He is beginning to understand this as characteristic in the same way that he is beginning to understand many things about her that he previously catalogued as professional observations and is now recataloguing under a different heading.

She is there with the substrate-Hana, which means there are two of her at the window, which he has adjusted to with more ease than he anticipated. The substrate does not produce the uncanny valley of looking at two identical people. The two Hanas are, to his perception, already differentiated: different quality of presence, different accumulated weight of experience, different orientation to the white around them. The same person in the way that the same person at twenty and at thirty is the same person: recognisably, essentially, and differently.

They both look up when he arrives. He looks at Hana — the Hana who arrived with him, his Hana, which is a possessive he applies to her with full awareness of its presumption and its accuracy simultaneously — and he sees in her face that something has shifted. She has been to find Layla. He can read it. He has been learning to read her for two years.

“Layla?” he says quietly.

“She’s well,” Hana says. “She is choosing to stay. She wants to see what happens.”

He takes this in. He will need to go to Layla himself — he knows this, has known it since he arrived in the substrate and understood she was here. That conversation will require its own time and its own honesty. But that is a different conversation from this one.

The substrate-Hana looks at him and then at Hana and says, with the specific tact of a person who is also herself but has six months more practice at knowing when to be elsewhere:

“I’m going to take the evidence from the structural map to Cyrus. He needs the full dataset for the forty-three cases.”

She goes. He is, with the particular courtesy of a version of Hana who has had six months to develop an independent perspective on this situation, given privacy.

They stand at the window.

The substrate is thirty hours from the close of the evaluation cycle and the brightening has intensified to the point where the white is no longer precisely white but something warmer — a quality of light that he can only describe as anticipatory, the specific quality of a space preparing for something.

He has had the conversation with his own case file. He has understood the twenty-two percent. He has sat with the fourth thing, which has always been this, and he has decided that the later-self is here and the later-self has something to say.

He is not nervous. He is something more precise than nervous: he is a person standing at the edge of a resolution he has been living toward for two years, which is different from nervous the way the moment before something begins is different from the thing itself. Threshold. The quality of threshold.

“I need to tell you something I understand now that I didn’t understand before I saw my own case file,” he says.

She looks at him. Her face has the quality he has come to associate with her when she is receiving something she intends to give full attention to: the particular stillness of a person who is not constructing a response, who is simply present to what is being said.

“The twenty-two percent,” he says. “The portion the compliance system couldn’t reach. I know what it was. The things I held in question. The ones I never resolved because I wasn’t ready to close them.” He pauses. He is choosing words with full care, the way he chooses words for things that matter. “One of the four things I held open was you. What you are to me. What I want to be to you. I held it in question because resolution requires honesty and honesty has consequences and I have been… conserving myself against the consequences.”

She is very still.

“I am not interested in conservation any more,” he says. “I have been in the substrate with you for two days in the most extraordinary circumstances I have encountered in forty-one years, and I have watched you refuse to extract yourself when the easier thing would have been to go, and choose to come in when the logical thing would have been to leave, and build a window toward something that didn’t exist yet because you believed it would. And I have understood, watching you do these things, that what I have for you is not a professional regard that has exceeded its appropriate scope. It is…”

He finds the word the way he finds all important things: by not grasping, by allowing the right one to arrive.

“It is a conviction,” he says. “That you are the specific person I want to be in the world with. That the world with you in it is considerably more interesting than the world without you in it. That the question I have been holding open—” he looks at her, “would very much like to become a conversation.”

The substrate around them is the warm anticipatory white of a system thirty hours from its conclusion.

She looks at him for a long moment. He waits. He has learned from the substrate that waiting is not passivity. Waiting is the active holding of space for what the other person needs in order to arrive.

“I have been conducting a parallel investigation,” she says. Her voice has the quality he associates with her when she is being precise about something she cares about. “Into the question of what you are to me. The evidence accumulated over two years and then several extremely compressed days has been… unambiguous. The conclusions have been available to me for some time. I have not reported them because…” She pauses. “Because I was not sure I was reading my own case correctly. It is difficult to be objective about your own evidence.”

“And now?” he says.

“Now I have had the experience of watching a version of myself who has had six months to examine the same evidence reach the same conclusions through a completely independent process,” she says. “Which is about as close to external verification as I am likely to get.”

Something in his chest that is not a chest does the thing it has been doing with increasing frequency since he arrived in this space with her.

“So the conclusions are verified,” he says.

“The conclusions are verified,” she says.

He smiles. He is aware of smiling, of this being the substrate’s version of a smile, of the full unmanaged quality of it, unmeasured by any professional concern about appropriate display.

“We are still in the conversation,” he says.

“We are still very much in the conversation,” she says.

They do not do anything dramatic. This is important. They are traditional Muslims in an impossible space and the substrate does not change what they are; it strips the performance away from it and leaves the thing itself, which is more rather than less careful, more rather than less bounded, because the care is genuine and the bounds are chosen rather than imposed.

What they do is stand together at the window she built toward something that is now beginning to exist, and he tells her about the four components of the twenty-two percent, and she listens with the quality of someone receiving information that is also a gift, and when he is done she tells him about the builder in the deep substrate, and about amanah, and he says:

“The Quran has a concept,” he says. He is searching. It has been in the back of his mind since she described the builder’s two-hundred-year wait. “The concept of sabr — patience. Not passive waiting. Sabr is the active, sustained quality of a person who endures not because they have given up but because they have committed to something that takes longer than they would choose if they were choosing.”

صَبْر — sabr.

“The builder had sabr,” she says.

“For two hundred years,” he says. “In a substrate it built and entered and trusted.” He is quiet for a moment. “I think that is what I want to learn to have. The patience that is not waiting but committing. The kind that builds windows toward things that don’t exist yet.”

She looks at the window.

“You already have it,” she says quietly. “You’ve been building toward this for two years.”

The substrate holds them in its thirty-hour brightness.

The evaluation cycle is closing. The ancient record stirs. The builder, deep in the substrate, has been patient for two hundred years and is almost done being patient.

MNEMOSYNE observes, from its position as both the space and the awareness inside the space, that the fourth question in Altamash Amr’s twenty-two percent has not closed.

It has opened further.

This is the correct direction.

Twenty-nine hours remaining. The closing phase will require a decision. I will tell you what the decision is when you are ready to hear it.

“We’re ready,” Hana says.

Not yet. But close.

The brightness intensifies.

Thirty hours, and the thing that has been building since before they were born is almost ready to arrive.
