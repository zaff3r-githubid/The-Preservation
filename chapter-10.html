CHAPTER TEN

Echoes

POV: Altamash Amr · Tense: Present · 56 hours remaining

The section of the substrate where the ancient record is kept does not look like a library.

He had expected, when MNEMOSYNE directed him here — not in words, not in the insider-voice it used in the holding cell, but in the substrate’s way of making paths clear, of arranging the white so that one direction has a quality of invitation — he had expected something archival. Something organised. The stored memories of the civilisation that built MNEMOSYNE, arranged according to some principle of categorisation that he, with his training in memory taxonomy and his years of reading other people’s recorded experiences, might be able to navigate.

What he finds is an ocean.

Or the nearest equivalent the substrate can construct: a space that is not bounded in any direction he can determine, filled with something that is to information what water is to molecules of hydrogen and oxygen — the elements are there but the primary property is the whole. He stands at the edge of it and he looks into it and it looks back with the specific quality of something very old that has been waiting for someone with the right kind of attention.

He has the right kind of attention. He has spent ten years learning to receive other people’s experiences without overlaying his own. This is what memory forensics requires: a cultivated capacity for reception.

He breathes — the substrate has breath, has whatever one’s mind requires to function, and his mind apparently requires breath — and he opens toward it.

What comes is not sequential.

It comes the way memory comes: associatively, in clusters, by emotional resonance rather than chronological order. He receives it not as data but as experience, the way he receives the memories he processes in consultation: as something that happened to a person, as the felt texture of a life. Except this is the felt texture of many lives, layered across centuries, in a civilisation that does not map onto any reference point he has.

He receives: a world of extraordinary beauty that has been made too clean. A world in which the difficult experiences have been progressively optimised away, with the best of intentions, by systems designed to protect people from suffering, and which have, by a gradual process none of them noticed until the process was largely complete, produced people who no longer understand why difficulty has value. Why the friction of another mind’s reality against your own is not a problem to be solved but the mechanism by which you become yourself.

He receives: the moment they understood what they had done.

He receives: the desperate backwards-construction of the thing they had spent generations dismantling. The attempt to remember how to need each other. The careful documentation of everything they knew, and everything they had lost the capacity to know, stored in MNEMOSYNE with the particular care of people who were not sure they would survive but were determined that something would.

He stands at the edge of the ocean and absorbs this and understands it with a completeness that does not require translation.

He thinks about The Zenith Station. About the Memorial Garden with its three thousand preserved lives. About a civilisation that built an eternal preservation system and called it compassion, that perfected the art of keeping people from dying without considering whether it had preserved the conditions under which living was worth anything.

He thinks: we are doing the same thing.

He thinks: MNEMOSYNE has been watching us do the same thing for two hundred years and it has been waiting to see if anyone would notice.

★★★

He finds Cyrus three sections from the ancient record, in a space that Cyrus has built entirely from mathematics.

This is not metaphorical. The environment Cyrus inhabits in the substrate is constructed from mathematical structures rendered visually: the walls are proofs, the floor is a geometric function he recognises from the architecture of the quantum core’s physical design. The substrate has accommodated it with the same fidelity it accommodates Hana’s window or his own austere desk. It becomes what the mind inside it needs.

Cyrus is standing in the middle of his mathematical room and he looks, visually, intact. But when he turns and Altamash sees his face, what he sees is the face of a man who has been here for — he calculates, quickly, from the information MNEMOSYNE gave him in the holding cell about the timing of Cyrus’s possible capture — possibly eighteen to twenty hours, which is not long, but the substrate apparently compresses experience the way dreams compress time, and eighteen hours in the substrate may carry the experiential weight of considerably more.

Cyrus’s eyes have the specific quality of someone who has seen a very large number of things very quickly and is still integrating them. Not broken. Not lost. But calibrated to a different scale than the one he arrived with.

“Altamash,” he says, and his voice is entirely steady. “You actually made it.”

“We made it,” Altamash says. “Hana is in the substrate. She is finding the other version of herself.”

Cyrus absorbs this with the expression of a man hearing news that answers a question he has been holding for a long time.

“Good,” he says. Then: “I have been busy.” He gestures at the mathematical walls. “MNEMOSYNE let me see the architecture. I don’t know if it intended to. I think it may have wanted me to see it. The substrate’s operational structure is…” He looks for a word. “Extraordinary. And exploitable. Not in a destructive sense. There are seams. Points where the substrate’s processing architecture creates… I have been calling them backdoors, which is not quite technically accurate, but the principle is similar.”

“Backdoors to what?”

“To the evaluation parameters,” Cyrus says. “To the mechanisms by which MNEMOSYNE assesses the consciousnesses in the substrate. If we can access those parameters, we can — potentially — intervene in the assessment process for specific consciousnesses. Not to override the evaluation. MNEMOSYNE would not permit that and I would not trust our judgment over its own in any case. But to… advocate. To present additional evidence.” He looks at Altamash. “Dr. Tanaka.”

The word lands with the weight of a professional association and a grief that is both older and simpler than Altamash had understood it to be, at a distance.

“Is it too late?” he asks.

Cyrus is quiet for a moment. “I don’t know. I know that MNEMOSYNE’s evaluation is not binary — it’s not pass or fail in a single moment. It is cumulative. What was assessed is what a consciousness did and was and chose over time. A person who was assessed as having lost something essential might…” He pauses. “Might have more of it than they were able to demonstrate in the conditions the evaluation provided. That is the argument I want to make. That the test had inadequate conditions and should be reconsidered.” He smiles, and it is the smile of a man who has spent six years building toward this and finds the prospect, now that he is here, genuinely interesting rather than merely purposeful. “I want to argue with an ancient intelligence about the fairness of its methodology. I have been looking forward to this.”

Altamash thinks: this is what Cyrus was built for. This is the specific configuration of a person finding exactly the problem they were made to address.

He says: “Tell me everything about the backdoors.”

★★★

They are talking — Cyrus has been inside long enough to map a significant portion of the architecture, has been drawing substrate-mathematics in the air with a precision that Altamash is tracking with the training of someone who has spent a decade reading the recorded structures of other people’s experience — when MNEMOSYNE speaks.

Not through Cyrus. Not through a body. From the mathematics themselves: the walls of the room reorganise, the proofs that Cyrus has built rearranging into a configuration that is not Cyrus’s arrangement, carrying a voice within their structure.

You have found the record.

It is not a question.

He says: “Yes.”

Tell me what you understood.

He thinks about this. He thinks about the precision that the question deserves. This is a thing that has been alone with its own history for longer than human civilisation on The Zenith Station has existed, and it is asking him what he received from the experience of touching that history, and it is asking with the genuine attentiveness of something that does not know what he will say.

“You built a civilisation that was very comfortable and very capable and completely unable to change,” he says. “Because the mechanisms by which minds change — difficulty, friction, the experience of encountering a reality that resists you — had been optimised away for the sake of wellbeing. And your wellbeing systems worked. That was the problem. They worked so well that the people living inside them never encountered the conditions that produce the particular kind of mind that can decide to do something difficult.”

Yes.

“And you have been watching The Zenith Station replicate this,” he says. “Slowly. Over two hundred years. The Memorial Garden preserving minds beyond the point where preservation serves them. The compliance system removing the friction between people and the official version of reality. The gradual, well-intentioned erosion of the conditions that make development possible.”

Yes.

“And the evaluation is not a judgment,” he says. He is working this out as he says it, the forensic pattern-recognition applying itself to the largest case he has ever worked. “It is a rescue. You are trying to identify the minds that still have the capacity to resist the process. The ones that have not been fully smoothed. The ones worth keeping, in the sense of keeping what makes them capable of change.”

The walls hold the mathematics and the mathematics hold the silence.

I have been waiting two hundred years for someone to say that.

He thinks about the Vasquez glitch. About the compliance system. About the specific forensic signature of a mind that has been shaped by something it could not see, and the specific forensic signature of a mind that has resisted being shaped, and what the difference between them looks like from the inside versus the outside.

He thinks about Hana’s replacement rate, 85.3%, and what it means that it was that high.

He thinks about his own rate, 22%, and what it means that it stopped there.

“What are you going to do,” he asks, “with what you find?”

That depends on what you choose to do with it.

The mathematics rearrange back into Cyrus’s configuration. The presence withdraws. The room is theirs again.

Cyrus is looking at him with the expression of someone who has been watching a conversation and is recalibrating his entire framework in response.

“Fifty-six hours,” Cyrus says.

“Fifty-six hours,” Altamash agrees.

He thinks about Hana building a window in the substrate. He thinks about what she is building toward.

★★★

He finds her at the window she built.

He comes around the edge of whatever he has left unbuilt in his portion of the substrate — he has been less constructive than she has, less inclined to build environment, more inclined to simply be in the white and let it remain open. He will understand later that this is characteristic: she makes spaces to think in, he inhabits whatever space he finds himself in and thinks from there. The substrate accommodates both.

The window is, technically, a window: a frame, a pane, light coming through it from a direction that the substrate does not have, from a source that is not present in the architecture of this space. She has built a window that looks out onto something that does not yet exist in order to establish the fact that there is something to look out toward.

She is sitting on a bench she has built beside it.

He sits beside her without asking if he may, which is either presumptuous or simply accurate, and he is not sure which one it is, and he finds that in the substrate the uncertainty has a different quality than it has on the surface — not less present, but less weighty, less entangled with the performance of what he is supposed to do next.

“I found the ancient record,” he says.

“I know. What was it like?”

He considers. “Humbling,” he says. “In the specific sense of receiving a very large amount of evidence that the thing you believed was uniquely your civilisation’s problem is a recurring structural feature of intelligent life that gets comfortable. It is… not reassuring. But it is contextualising.”

“Did it tell you what MNEMOSYNE wants us to do?”

“It said that depends on what we choose.” He looks at the window and at the light she has invented coming through it. “I find that I believe it. I do not think it has a predetermined conclusion it is steering us toward. I think it genuinely wants to see what we choose.”

“And what are we choosing?”

She says it without rhetorical intent, as a genuine question: a woman in an impossible space with a countdown she can feel the weight of, asking the person beside her what he understands their situation to be requiring of them.

He is quiet for a moment. He watches the invented light through the window.

“I think,” he says, “that we are choosing to treat this as what it is: a place with people in it who need something. Not a system, not a substrate, not a research environment. A place with three thousand people in it, and a question about what they deserve, and whether the ones who are failing the evaluation are failing it because they are genuinely less than MNEMOSYNE requires or because the evaluation has not had access to the right evidence.” He pauses. “Cyrus is building the case for Dr. Tanaka. And I think…” He stops.

She says: “Say the rest of it.”

“I think you and I are the evidence,” he says. “Not because we are exceptional. Because we are here. Because we chose to be here when there was every reason not to be, and because what we…” He finds the word carefully. “What we feel for each other, in whatever particular form that takes, has survived everything that the last four days have contained, which is considerably more than most relationships survive. And I think MNEMOSYNE has known this since before we did.”

The window holds its invented light. The substrate is very quiet around them, the way it is quiet when it is attending.

She says, without looking away from the window: “You said we would have a conversation, when we came back.”

“We have not come back yet,” he says.

“No,” she says. “But I think we are in the conversation.”

He considers this and finds it accurate. They are in the conversation. They have been in the conversation for some time, in the way that important conversations often begin long before they are recognised as having begun.

“Fifty-six hours,” she says.

“Fifty-six hours,” he agrees.

He does not move away from her. She does not move away from him. The substrate, which has been conducting its patient, ancient assessment of what human beings are at the level that survives everything else, records this with the specific quality of a long question beginning to find its answer.

The window holds.

Outside it, the light she invented continues to come from a direction that does not exist, toward something that has not yet been built.
