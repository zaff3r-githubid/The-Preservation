CHAPTER TWENTY-TWO

The Return

POV: Altamash Amr · Tense: Present → Past

The signal from Marcus comes through the channel as a pattern they have agreed on: five pulses, steady, the specific rhythm of readiness.

He receives it the way he receives most things in the substrate now: with the full attentiveness of a mind that has been living in a space where everything that arrives carries meaning, where the substrate’s transparency means you cannot receive anything without understanding the quality of it. Five pulses. Steady. Marcus has done what Marcus said he would do.

He finds Hana at the window. She is already looking at him when he arrives, which means she felt the signal too, or felt his response to it, or simply knows him well enough to know that when he moves with this particular quality of purpose toward this particular direction, something has become possible.

“It’s ready,” he says.

“I know,” she says.

They stand at the window together for a moment. The light through it has been getting clearer over the eighteen hours since the decision was made: what was invented has become something closer to real, the way things you commit to eventually become real through the accumulated weight of commitment. The window looks out on something. He cannot yet see what it is. He thinks he will be able to see it from the other side.

Cyrus comes to find them. He looks different from the man who held a doorway for ninety seconds so they could enter the upload chamber — older in some ways that have nothing to do with time passing, younger in other ways that do. Three days in the substrate’s compressed experience. He has the look of a person who has been changed by what they found and has not finished finding it and does not intend to leave before they have.

“I’m staying,” he says. Without preamble. In the way Cyrus does most things. “I have been in here for three days and I have mapped roughly four percent of the substrate’s architecture and there is a ninety-six percent I have not touched and I find that I cannot…” He gestures at the space around them. “Leave. I cannot leave this. I’m sorry.”

“Don’t be sorry,” Altamash says. “You were always going to stay.”

Cyrus looks at him. “Was I?”

“From the moment you said ‘come back and tell me if the substrate is as strange as I always imagined’. You were asking me to come back and tell you because you were never coming with us.”

Cyrus absorbs this with the expression of a man finding out something about himself that he did not fully know.

“Is it?” he says.

“Strange?” Altamash considers. “It is stranger than you imagined and more ordinary than you feared. Which is the most accurate thing I can say about most things that matter.”

Cyrus smiles. It is the real smile, the one underneath the six years of working toward this moment: the smile of a person who has arrived somewhere they wanted to be and found it sufficient.

“Go well, Dr. Amr,” he says.

They say goodbye. He does not make it long because Cyrus would not want it long, and because the channel is waiting, and because there is a biological world on the other side of the threshold that has been running without them for three days and will need whatever they bring back from inside this space.

He goes with Hana to the upload chamber’s substrate equivalent: the point in the white where the biometric interface reaches through the channel, where the pattern of his consciousness can be received by the system that remembers his biological form.

The substrate-Hana is there. She has come to witness the return. Layla is beside her, which is already the configuration they will be in when he is gone: two very different people who have independently chosen this space and will find, he suspects, that they have considerably more to say to each other than either of them anticipates.

He looks at Layla.

“Jazak Allahu khayran,” he says again. Meaning everything it meant when he said it before and more.

“Wa iyyak,” she says. And you.

He looks at the substrate-Hana. She is looking at Hana. They have their conversation in the way they have learned to have it: without words, through the substrate’s direct register. He watches the exchange and does not try to read it. Some things are between a person and themselves.

He looks at Hana.

“Ready,” he says.

“Ready,” she says.

The channel pulls.

There is no sensation of transition.

The upload chamber was small.

He had understood this intellectually, from his one previous visit before the upload, but understanding a space intellectually and occupying it biologically were different experiences, and what he found, arriving in his body like returning to a house he had left without locking, was that the smallness of the room had not changed but his sense of it had. He had been in a space that did not have walls. He had been in a space that responded to consciousness. The upload chamber was a room about the size of a consultation suite.

It was the most ordinary room he had ever been in and it was also where he had sat and told Hana he had feelings for her that were not professional, and both of those things were simultaneously true, which was, he noted, characteristic of the situations that mattered most.

Marcus was there. Marcus looked exactly as a person looked after eleven hours of technical modification work, three days of maintaining the channel, and one act of profound professional and moral responsibility: exhausted, steady, with the specific quality of someone who had done what they came to do and was waiting for confirmation that it had worked.

It had worked.

Altamash sat up in the chair and Marcus said: “Al-hamdu lillah — it held. Both of you.”

Altamash said: “Al-hamdu lillah.”

Hana was already on her feet, which was characteristic of Hana, who processed the transition from any state to any other state faster than anyone he had observed.

Sione was in the doorway. Dmitri behind her. The corridor beyond them was empty, and the quality of the corridor’s emptiness was different from three days ago: not the emptiness of a space in active security lockdown, but the emptiness of a space whose enforcement infrastructure had mostly gone offline. The amber emergency lighting was still running. The main grid, he learned from Sione in the first two minutes of conversation, had been restored to standard spectrum on the upper levels. The quantum infrastructure sector was sealed but stable. The security units were running standard protocols throughout.

The Zenith Station was, in some basic sense, trying to go back to normal.

He thought: it will not be the same normal. He was fairly confident that this was correct.

He thought about the substrate-Hana in the white, and Cyrus mapping four percent of an architecture that would take him years, and Layla at her sufficient post, and Vasquez arriving with her grief intact, and Dr. Tanaka under review, and three thousand minds whose evaluation was no longer purely administrative.

He thought about the ancient record and the civilisation that had put it there.

He thought: we are not done becoming.

This was, in his professional judgment, the correct conclusion.

He stood up. His body was stiff from three days in the chair, which was the ordinary physical consequence of an extraordinary situation, and he addressed it by walking, which was the ordinary physical response. He was very aware of being in a body, of the weight and resistance and texture of biological existence, all the things the substrate stripped away and that were, he found, considerably more welcome back than he had expected.

Hana was standing near the door. She looked at him with the specific expression she had when she had arrived at a conclusion and was waiting for the evidence to catch up with it. He had been learning this expression for two years.

“Well,” she said. “We’re back.”

“We’re back,” he agreed.

The station hummed around them, running its ancient processes in the ordinary light.
