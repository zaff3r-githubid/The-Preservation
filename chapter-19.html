CHAPTER NINETEEN

Altamash Goes to Layla

POV: Altamash Amr · Tense: Present · 22 hours remaining

He has been putting this off.

He knows he has been putting it off. He has the self-awareness to name it: there is something in the prospect of this conversation that produces the specific resistance of approaching something that will hurt in ways he cannot fully predict, and that will also be necessary, and that he has owed since the moment he arrived in the substrate and understood she was here.

He has been in the substrate for two and a half days. Layla has been here for three years. She saw the announcement. She was in the room when the builder spoke. She has been carrying, for three years, the weight of something she loved and released, and he has been walking around the substrate processing his own twenty-two percent and building his case for the argument and having the conversation at the window, and he has not come to her.

He goes now.

The room built from his memory is exactly as Hana described: his memory, his composition, his attention visible in every angle of it. He recognises it the moment he enters. He recognises exactly the moment the memory comes from, which is the evening he understood, with the clarity of something that had been true for long enough to become undeniable, that what he felt for Hana was not what he felt for Layla, and that the difference was not a hierarchy but a different category altogether, and that knowing this meant he could not continue as he had been continuing.

He had not said anything that evening. He had sat in this room, on this chair, with Layla across from him, and he had held the knowledge like a stone in his chest and said nothing because he did not yet know how to say it without it being a wound.

She uploaded three months after that evening. He had not yet said anything. He had been waiting for the right moment with the specific patience of a person who suspects the right moment may not exist.

She died, in the biological sense, four months into the upload. Not degradation — the upload took, the consciousness survived, she has been here for three years — but the body she left behind completed its cycle, and he attended the memorial, and he said, in the memorial space, all the things he had not said while she was biological.

She has been here for three years, knowing, because the substrate gives access to everything a consciousness holds about another mind, that he said those things after she was gone.

He enters the room and she is there. Of course she is there. This is her home.

“You came,” she says.

“I should have come sooner.”

“You came when you were ready. That’s the same as sooner, in the substrate.” She looks at him with the three-years-familiar quality that he finds, now that he is inside it, more nourishing than it has any right to be. She knows him. She has known him for a long time. Whatever the substrate-sharing has added to that knowledge, the foundation is years of genuine regard, and it is entirely present in the way she looks at him. “Sit down, Altamash.”

He sits. The chair is the one from his memory and it fits him in the way that things you remember fit you: not quite perfectly, because you have changed since the memory was made, but close.

“I know what I owe you,” he says. He has decided to be direct because Layla has always preferred directness and three years in the substrate have presumably not changed that. “I owe you the honesty I didn’t have in time. The things I didn’t say.”

“I heard them,” she says. “At the memorial.”

“You shouldn’t have had to hear them at the memorial.”

She is quiet for a moment. “No,” she agrees. “But I did hear them. And they were true. And the fact that they came late doesn’t make them less true. It just makes them…” She searches. “Makes them the version of the truth that a person tells when they run out of reasons to wait.”

He takes this in. It is not absolution exactly. It is something more honest than absolution: a description of what happened, offered without anger, which requires more grace than forgiveness and is considerably harder to produce.

“Hana told me about your conversation,” he says.

“I know. She would.” A small pause. “She is good, you know. Not perfect. She is forty-one percent her own person and fifty-nine percent the compliance system’s intervention, and the forty-one percent is extraordinary and the fifty-nine percent is going to take years of undoing. But she is…” Layla looks at the room, the chair, the specific light. “She is the kind of person who builds windows toward things that don’t exist yet and then waits for them to exist. That is the best kind of person. You need that.”

“Layla.”

“I’m not doing you a favour,” she says, with a sharpness that is not unkind. “I am stating a thing that is true. You need someone who builds toward things. You have always been the person who holds things in question, and that is essential, that is the twenty-two percent, but a person who only holds things in question without someone beside them pointing toward the horizon gets…” She makes a gesture. “Stuck in the open question. She points. You hold it open. Together that’s a whole mind.”

He looks at her. He thinks: three years in this space, and what it has produced in her is clarity. The substrate stripped the performance from everything and what is left is Layla, the essential Layla, knowing exactly what she thinks and saying it.

“What do you want?” he asks. The same question Hana asked her.

“I already told Hana.”

“Tell me.”

She looks at her hands. Then at him. “I want to be useful,” she says. “Not in the sense of a function performed. In the sense of a person whose presence in a space makes the space better. I have been useful in this substrate, I think, as a subject of study. MNEMOSYNE found my case interesting. The builder witnessed me. That is something. But what I want, going forward —” She pauses. “I want the other Hana to have company.”

He is still.

“If she stays,” Layla says. “I don’t know what she will decide. But if she stays in the substrate — if she chooses to remain and work from inside, as a collaborator rather than a subject — she will be the only living human consciousness in a system that is otherwise three thousand uploaded people who are all dealing with their own evaluation outcomes. She will need someone who knows what the substrate is, who is neither a subject nor MNEMOSYNE, who can simply… be here with her.” She looks at him. “I have been here three years. I know this space. I know how to be small in it without losing myself. I think I can be what she needs.”

“That’s not a small thing,” he says. “You understand that. Staying here indefinitely.”

“I know what I’m choosing,” she says. “I have been choosing it since I arrived. I just didn’t know what I was choosing it for.” She smiles, and it is the real smile, the one he remembers from years of shared dinners and professional crossings and the specific warmth of a person who finds the people they love genuinely interesting. “Now I do.”

He has no adequate response to this. He knows he has no adequate response to this and he does not try to manufacture one. He sits in the chair that is built from his memory and he lets what he is feeling be visible, which is the substrate’s version of the thing he has spent forty-one years managing behind professionalism.

“Jazak Allahu khayran,” he says.

جَزَاكَ اللهُ خَيْرًا — Jazak Allahu khayran.

May God reward you with goodness.

Layla looks at him for a moment and then says, with the quiet precision of someone returning an offering: “Wa iyyak.” And you.

وَإِيَّاكَ — Wa iyyak.

He stays a while longer. They talk about things that are not the evaluation or the closing phase or the twenty-two percent or anything that carries a specific weight: she tells him about three years of watching the substrate and the small observations she has accumulated, the peculiar way that grief resolves in this space, the way some consciousnesses have built impossible architectures of beauty that no one on the biological Station has ever seen. He tells her about the ancient record and what it felt like to stand at the edge of the ocean of a civilisation’s accumulated experience and feel it look back.

It is an ordinary conversation. In the most extraordinary of circumstances, it is simply two people talking, and this is perhaps the thing the substrate has given him that he had least expected: the capacity to be ordinary inside the impossible.

He says goodbye, which is not permanent, because he will come back and she will be here. He takes this with him as he leaves: the specific comfort of knowing she will be here, she will be well, she has chosen what she has chosen and it is, as she said, the right size.

[AUTHOR'S NOTE: Chapter 19 is a chapter of debt paid and gift given. Altamash’s visit to Layla is the emotional obligation the novel has been carrying since Ch16 when Hana went first. The key beats: his acknowledgement of what he owes (not grovelling, not performed), her refusal to process it as a wound, her offer to companion substrate-Hana. The jazak Allahu khayran exchange is the correct register — the specific Islamic formula for gratitude that acknowledges the divine frame without being preachy. The final 'ordinary conversation' is deliberate: the substrate gives ordinary back. This is what has been missing from the Station for two hundred years.]
