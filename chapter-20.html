CHAPTER TWENTY

The Two-Way Channel

POV: Marcus Okafor · Tense: Past · Biological world

The modification took eleven hours.

Not because the technical work was difficult — Marcus had been working in the shadow of this problem for four years, and Vasquez’s instructions had been precise, and the extraction device’s quantum resonance architecture was, once he understood what he was looking at, elegant in a way that good tools always were: designed to do one thing exactly and to do it with the minimum number of moving parts. The difficulty was the testing. Every step of the modification needed to be verified against a physical outcome that could not be simulated in advance, and every verification required resetting the entire system, and twice he found errors that meant going back three steps and rebuilding the relevant component from scratch.

Sione and the others rotated on the corridor in four-hour shifts. The man with the physical-systems hands, whose name was Dmitri, came in twice to look over Marcus’s shoulder at the interface configuration and once made a suggestion that saved two hours. Marcus took the suggestion without ceremony. This was not a time for professional ego.

The Station around them was changing. He could feel it through the infrastructure: a quality in the ventilation systems, in the lighting, in the small ambient electronic textures of a place that had been running the same continuous process for two centuries and was now, component by component, running it differently. The compliance network’s enforcement nodes were going offline. The Station did not collapse when they went — the life support, the navigation, the basic institutional functions continued without interruption, because someone, long ago, had been sensible enough to build those systems on separate infrastructure. What the compliance nodes had controlled was narrower than people understood from the inside: the information people received about their own minds.

The station was the same station. The people in it were starting, gradually, to be less managed.

He thought about eleven years of compliance work and what it had felt like from the inside. He thought about the cases he had processed and the forms he had signed and the calibrations he had approved without, at the time, understanding what the approval meant. He thought about the four years of documentation he had been building as a private act of professional conscience.

He thought: it was not enough. It was also not nothing. He would have to live with both of those things simultaneously, which was, in his experience, the condition most of human moral life occupied.

He finished the modification. He tested it.

The interface accepted the extraction device’s resonance parameters.

The two-way channel opened.

★★★

The first signal from the substrate arrived eleven seconds after the channel opened. Not speech — the channel was not yet calibrated for the complexity of full consciousness transmission. What came through was simpler: a pattern, three pulses, a pause, two pulses. He recognised it. He had heard it described, in a different context — Altamash’s account of the vent communication in the holding cell corridor.

He tapped back. Two pulses, three pulses.

Three pulses, two pulses.

She was there.

He sat in the upload chamber and took a breath and allowed himself, for thirty seconds, to feel the full weight of what had just happened. He had been moving for eleven hours without stopping because stopping meant thinking, and thinking meant feeling the specific variety of feeling that he had learned, in his years of compliance work, to keep separate from operational function. The thirty seconds were sufficient.

Then he calibrated the channel for linguistic transmission and began the slow process of establishing a communication protocol.

★★★

Vasquez arrived at the upload chamber door two hours later.

He knew it was her because Sione stepped inside to warn him thirty seconds before she appeared: not the synchronised security pattern, not the MNEMOSYNE-driven coordination. A single figure, moving with the specific unsteadiness he had come to recognise as the intervals becoming shorter than the displacements.

She was barely there.

When she came through the door, what he saw was a Director in the last interval of a seven-month displacement — the body that had been used as a vessel for too long, showing the cost in every line of its posture and every movement that arrived slightly after the intention that should have preceded it. She was fighting to stay coherent in her own body with the effort of someone swimming against a current that had been stronger than her for months and was now simply exhausted.

She looked at the upload chamber. At the chairs. At the interface he had spent eleven hours modifying.

“Is it done?” she asked. Her voice was her own, with the quality of something that had been held under pressure for a very long time and was reaching the edge of what pressure could maintain.

“It’s done,” he said.

“Two-way.”

“Two-way,” he confirmed. He paused. “Director.”

She looked at him.

“Your body,” he said carefully. Not clinical, not evasive. The specific gentleness of someone who has been in compliance work for eleven years and has learned how to say difficult things. “How much time do you think it has?”

She did not look away. She looked at her left hand. The tell. The hand was shaking with the stuttered repetition that no longer had intervals between its occurrences.

“Not enough to matter,” she said. “For the body. The question is whether what’s in it is worth…” She stopped. She started again. “The grief is still mine. Whatever it took from me — seven months of decisions, of presence, of the ordinary texture of my own life — the grief it couldn’t reach is still mine. And I’m very tired of holding it in a body that isn’t mine any more.”

He looked at her for a long time.

The compliance system had a protocol for this situation. The protocol said: emergency medical intervention. The protocol said: restraint of the decision-making capacity of a person in a compromised state. The protocol said many things, and he had spent four years building a file that argued, quietly and comprehensively, that the protocol was wrong about what it said.

“Sit down,” he said.

She sat in the chair. The chair held her with the same consideration it held everyone who had sat in it, indifferent to the circumstances of arrival.

“Is there anything you want to do first?” he asked.

She thought about it. Her hand was still on the armrest. Then she said:

“I want to say the Shema.”

He had not known she was Jewish. He thought, now, that this was the kind of thing the compliance system had been very effective at making professionally irrelevant, which was one of its more successful diminishments.

“Then say it,” he said. He stepped back to give her the space of it.

She closed her eyes.

She said, quietly, in the ancient language: Shema Yisrael, Adonai Eloheinu, Adonai Echad. Hear, O Israel: the Lord our God, the Lord is One.

She opened her eyes.

“All right,” she said.

He ran the modified interface.

She went in alive, which the system had not been designed for and which the system handled anyway, because the two-way channel was built for minds that were fully present, and Vasquez was, in the last moment of the process, more fully present than she had been in seven months.

What was left in the chair was still.

He sat with it for a while. Then he covered it with the emergency blanket from the chamber’s supply kit, which was inadequate to the gravity of the moment and also the only thing available, and he considered that most of what humans offered in the face of death was inadequate to the gravity of the moment, and that this was not a reason to withhold the offering.

He went back to the interface. He had a channel to maintain.

[AUTHOR'S NOTE: Chapter 20 is Marcus’s culminating chapter and carries the novel’s second death — Vasquez, choosing the substrate while she still has enough of herself left to choose. The Shema is the right prayer for this moment: the fundamental declaration of Jewish faith, traditionally spoken at death, says exactly what Vasquez would say at the threshold between biological and digital existence. Marcus stepping back to give her the space of it is the most important thing he does in the novel. The compliance system protocol is mentioned and discarded without drama. 'He thought about what he had approved without understanding' is the closest the novel comes to an institutional reckoning, and it is deliberately quiet.]
