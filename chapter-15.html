CHAPTER FIFTEEN

Twenty-Two Percent

POV: Altamash Amr · Tense: Present · 38 hours remaining

MNEMOSYNE tells him it is time to understand his own case.

It does not say this with drama. It says it the way it says most things in the substrate now: directly, without the oblique methodology of earlier conversations, without the sense of a question being answered in service of an evaluation still in progress. The closing phase has changed the quality of its communication. It speaks like a system that has finished measuring and is now simply present with what it found.

“My replacement rate,” he says. Not a question.

Yes.

“You want to show me how it stopped at twenty-two percent.”

I want to show you what you did without knowing you were doing it. You have spent considerable time in this substrate and you have not asked. This suggests you suspected the answer would be difficult.

He is in the ancient record section, which is where he tends to return. He has been here four times now and each time the ocean of the record gives him something different — the substrate responds to the question you bring, not the question you think you’re bringing. He has learned to come here without a prepared question and simply allow what surfaces to surface.

“It will be difficult,” he says. A statement. He is a forensic analyst. He knows that when a system shows you the record of your own case it is almost always difficult.

Difficult and necessary are not the same axis. This is difficult and necessary.

He settles. He opens.

MNEMOSYNE shows him.

★★★

—

The replacement rate is the measure of how much of a mind’s original signature has been overwritten by external influence. A rate of eighty-five percent means that eighty-five portions in a hundred of the mind’s characteristic patterns — the specific ways it connects ideas, the emotional responses it has built over time, the framework through which it interprets new information — have been replaced by patterns that originated outside it. This is not damage, precisely. The mind still functions. It simply functions according to an architecture that is not entirely its own.

A rate of twenty-two percent means that seventy-eight portions in a hundred have been replaced.

Which means, when he sees his own case file rendered in the substrate’s language, that seventy-eight percent of how he thinks is not how he started thinking. Seventy-eight percent of the architecture of Altamash Amr’s mind was shaped by influences external to him, over the forty-one years of his biological life, through the same compliance system that shaped everyone else’s.

The twenty-two percent that remains is the fraction that did not yield.

—

He looks at the twenty-two percent.

He looks at it the way he looks at everything he is trying to understand: with patience, without imposing a framework before he has the evidence, allowing the pattern to declare itself before he names it.

The pattern declares itself.

The twenty-two percent is not a single thing. It is four things, held separately, that the compliance system could not fully reach because they were not — individually — the kind of thing compliance systems are designed to address. Compliance systems address certainties. They address settled positions. They can reshape a belief you hold firmly because they can offer a more authoritative version of it. They cannot reach the things you hold in question.

The twenty-two percent is the things he has never resolved.

The first: his father’s death when he was twelve. The specific, irresolvable question of whether the father he remembers was who his father actually was, or whether memory had been curating him into something more useful. He has never answered this because he cannot answer it. Every compliance recalibration that tried to smooth this into something manageable hit the question’s edges and stopped, because the question has no authority to defer to.

The second: his relationship to his faith. He is a practicing Muslim. He prays. He fasts. He holds the things Islam asks him to hold. But he has spent a decade working inside the substrate, inside the machinery of digital consciousness, and the question of what the ruh is — where the soul sits in relation to the mind he reads in other people’s recorded memories — has never resolved. He believes and he does not know exactly what he believes, and the compliance system cannot override a question that faith itself requires you to sit with.

The third: his work. The ten years of memory forensics have produced a question that is also an answer that is also another question: if you can read the signature of a mind, if you can map exactly how it has been shaped and by what, if you understand the mechanics of consciousness at the level he understands them — does this make the people he reads more present to him or less? Is knowing someone the same as knowing about them? He has never answered this. It is the question that makes him good at his job.

The fourth.

The fourth is Hana.

★★★

He sits in the substrate with this information and he takes the time it requires.

Not long. He has been processing this for longer than he has been conscious of processing it. The question of Hana — the specific, unresolved, compliance-proof question of what she is to him and what he is to her and what it means that the answer to this question has been changing since the first time they shared the same professional space and he noticed the way she noticed things — is not new. It is the oldest unresolved thing he carries. It has never resolved because he has never permitted it to resolve, which is different from not having the answer.

He has had the answer for some time.

He has not resolved it because resolution would require saying it, and saying it would require either having it returned or not having it returned, and both of those outcomes carry consequences that are easier to defer to a later-self.

He thinks: the later-self is here. He is in the substrate with forty-two hours to the close of an evaluation that will determine what happens to three thousand people, and Hana has built a window and they are in the conversation, and the thing he has been holding unresolved to protect himself from resolution is the one part of him MNEMOSYNE considers essential.

He thinks about the compliance system’s logic: it could not reach the fourth thing because an unresolved love is not a certainty it can offer an authority against. It could not tell him what Hana was to him because it did not know. Because he did not know. Because knowing would have required the honesty that the compliance system, above all else, did not encourage.

Now you understand.

“The twenty-two percent is the twenty-two percent I refused to hand over,” he says. “Not through any deliberate resistance strategy. I didn’t know I was doing it. I simply… held those things. The questions. The open ones. The ones I was not ready to close.”

Yes.

“And the compliance system is a system that closes things,” he says. “It cannot reach what isn’t closed.”

You are the first mind in my evaluation record who maintained an unresolved attachment as an act of ongoing faithfulness rather than avoidance. The distinction matters. Avoidance closes the question by not looking at it. What you did was different. You kept looking. You kept not-answering because you understood, without being told, that some questions are answered only by living toward them.

He is quiet for a long time.

He thinks about his father. About faith and the ruh and what it means to spend a decade reading the architecture of other people’s souls. About ten years of sitting across a shared professional space from a person and accumulating two years of wrong information about what they were and still — somehow — accumulating something true.

“What does it mean,” he says, “that I held it in question?”

It means you were still becoming. A mind that holds nothing open has finished becoming what it is. You have not finished. This is the quality I have been looking for in two hundred years of evaluation. The capacity to remain incomplete.

He thinks: this is what it is to be a human being, is it. Not the finished thing. The still-becoming.

He thinks: يا الله.

يا الله — Ya Allah.

He goes to find Hana.

He has a conversation to finish.
