INTERLUDE

The Dream

POV: Hana · Tense: Present · Substrate · Between Ch15 and Ch16

The dream gives her a house.

Not the Station’s white corridors, not the substrate’s luminous space — a house, with rooms that have accumulated the particular disorder of a life fully inhabited: books stacked on window ledges, a prayer rug folded over the arm of a chair, a kitchen that smells of something slow-cooked and warming, mornings that arrive through actual windows carrying actual light.

She does not know the city. It does not matter.

What she knows, in the specific way you know things in dreams — not as information but as texture, as atmosphere, as the quality of warmth that precedes waking — is that this is hers. That she has been here before. That the sound of movement in the kitchen, the particular rhythm of someone who knows where everything is without looking, is a sound she has fallen asleep to and woken to for long enough that she cannot remember the room without it.

She lies in the light and she listens.

He is making tea. She knows this without seeing it: the specific sequence of his movements, the particular sound he makes when the kettle is slow — a quiet exhale of patience, not quite a sigh — are things she has mapped the way she maps evidence, the way she maps any system she has lived inside long enough to understand. She knows when he will appear in the doorway. She counts the seconds.

He appears in the doorway.

He is holding two cups and he is already looking at her with the expression she has learned means he has been aware she was awake for some time and has said nothing — which is the thing he does. He notices things and holds them quietly, giving her the space to arrive at them herself.

“You were counting,” he says.

“I wasn’t.”

“Twelve seconds. You always count from the second whistle.”

She sits up. The light is unreasonable — the kind of morning light that seems to have organised itself specifically for the purpose of landing on him in a way that is entirely unfair — and she accepts the tea and looks at him, and he sits on the edge of the bed and looks back, and there is nothing between them that requires management.

This is the thing the dream keeps returning to. Not the grand moments but this: the specific quality of a life in which she does not have to be careful. In which the complete version of herself — the one the compliance system has never been able to fully reach — is the thing he has married. Is the thing he wakes up next to. Is the thing he brings tea to at twelve seconds past the second whistle without requiring it to be smaller or quieter or more convenient.

He says something that makes her laugh. She cannot, afterward, reconstruct what it was — the dream dissolves the content and preserves the sensation: the warmth of it moving through her without warning, the way he looks when he has made her laugh, as though this is the specific thing he was assembled for.

“Stop,” she says, still laughing.

“I’m not doing anything.”

“You’re looking.”

“I’m allowed to look.”

He says this with the simplicity of someone stating a geographic fact. Not a claim. A condition of the world as currently arranged.

She turns toward the window. The city below is doing its morning things — she can hear it, sense it, feel the vast ordinary ongoing life of a place that does not know she is watching it. She feels the weight of his attention on the side of her face the way you feel warmth from a lamp: diffuse, constant, requiring nothing.

His hand — she has not heard him move — comes to rest on her hair. Not a gesture with an agenda. Just a hand. The specific and unhurried communication of: I am here, and here is where I intend to be, and there is nowhere I am trying to get to.

ال۰حمْدُ للۼہ, she thinks.

All praise for this. For the ordinary specific weight of this. For the tea going cold because neither of them has moved to drink it. For the laughing, for the looking, for the twelve seconds from the second whistle, for the prayer rug over the arm of the chair in a house that smells of something warming.

She turns back to him.

He is watching her with an expression she does not have a word for in either of the languages she thinks in — so she has given it a name only she knows: the expression that means he has found her, specifically and exactly her, and is not done finding her, and does not expect to be.

“What?” she says.

“Nothing.”

The way you say nothing when you mean: everything, and I have enough time to say it, and you already know.

She rests her forehead against his shoulder. The city continues its morning. The tea continues cooling. The light continues its unreasonable performance. She breathes, and he breathes, and the dream holds them there — in the domesticity and the laughter and the specific mercy of a life in which nothing needs to be managed — for as long as it can.

★★★

She feels it beginning to thin at the edges. The way dreams thin: not a sudden erasure but a gentling, a slow releasing, the way you set something down with care rather than dropping it.

She does not try to hold it.

She has learned, somewhere in the substrate’s long white months, that the things worth holding hold themselves.

She wakes.

The substrate’s light replaces the dream’s light and her hand is open in the space where his shoulder was. She lies still for a moment, taking inventory of what remains: the warmth, the laugh she cannot reconstruct, the weight of his hand in her hair, the expression with no name.

Enough, she thinks.

More than enough.

[AUTHOR'S NOTE: Placement: between Ch15 and Ch16.
